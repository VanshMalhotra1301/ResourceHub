Priority Scheduling (Non-Preemptive)

In this algorithm, the OS assigns a priority to each process. The CPU is allocated to the process with the highest priority in the Ready Queue. Convention: A lower number indicates a higher priority (e.g., Priority 1 is higher than Priority 5).

Priority Scheduling Theory

Characteristics

  • Criteria: Priority Number. Lower numbers generally represent higher priorities in academia (e.g., UNIX priority).
  • Mode: Non-Preemptive. Once executing, a process runs until it finishes its burst.
  • Tie-Breaker: If two processes share the same priority, Arrival Time (FCFS) determines who goes first.

The Starvation Problem

A major flaw of Priority Scheduling is Starvation. A steady stream of high-priority processes can leave a low-priority process waiting indefinitely in the Ready Queue.

The Solution: Aging

To fix starvation, Operating Systems implement Aging: a technique where the priority of a waiting process gradually increases the longer it sits in the Ready Queue, guaranteeing it will eventually execute.
